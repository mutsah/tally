A quiet ledger for one person's money

Self-hosted personal finance tracking. Record what comes in, what goes out, and what you hold — Tally shows where the money goes, what's losing value, and how much you actually keep.

What Tally is

Money you can see clearly

Tally is a single-owner tracker you run on your own server. Everything is entered by hand, on purpose — no bank sync to break, no ads, no AI telling you what to do. Just an honest picture of your accounts.

Principle

Manual by design

You type what happened. That friction is the feature — it keeps you close to your money instead of skimming a feed.

Principle

One currency

USD only. No exchange rates, no conversion drift, no dual-currency reporting to reconcile.

Principle

Yours to host

Runs on your own VM behind your own TLS. Your ledger never leaves infrastructure you control.

Principle

Truth over guesses

Every number on the dashboard is computed from what you recorded. Nothing is estimated or inferred.

The stack

Built to last, small enough to own

A NestJS API and a Next.js front end share a Postgres database through Prisma. The whole thing ships as Docker Compose behind Caddy — deployable to a single always-free ARM VM.

Where the line is

What v1 does, and what it deliberately doesn't

v1 is the tracking spine — everything needed to record money and read it back. Anything that adds modelling complexity waits for a later version with a clear reason.

In v1
  • Accounts of every kind, cash-flow or manually valued
  • Income and expense transactions against categories
  • Transfers between your own accounts
  • Editable categories with one level of nesting
  • A dashboard: net worth, spending, savings rate
  • Filtered CSV export of your transactions
  • Single-owner accounts on a multi-tenant foundation
Not in v1
  • Bank connections or automatic imports
  • Any currency other than USD
  • Budgets — those arrive in v2
  • Lot-level investment tracking — v3
  • Shared household or organisation books
  • Mobile apps — the web UI is responsive instead

One owner today, many tenants tomorrow

Tally is used by one person, but it is multi-tenant from the first migration. Every domain row carries a userId from day one, and per-user isolation is enforced in application code now. Postgres row-level security policies are written in v1 but left dormant; v4 simply switches them on. That means opening Tally to other people later is additive — never a rewrite.

The model

Four ideas hold the whole app together

Accounts, categories, transactions, and valuations. Get these right and everything the dashboard shows falls out of them cleanly.

Accounts come in two flavours

Flow-derived accounts — cash, bank — carry a balance that is the running sum of their transactions. Money is real because you can trace every dollar.

Manually valued accounts — investments, the microloan book — hold a value you can't compute from receipts. Their balance is the latest snapshot you record, nothing else.

Transfers are movement, not spending

Moving money between your own accounts isn't income and isn't an expense. In Tally a transfer is a single record with a source and a destination and no category.

Because of that, it never lands in a spending report and never double-counts your net worth. The money just changed pockets.

Categories: a starter set you're free to bend

New accounts begin seeded with fourteen sensible categories. Keep them, rename them, delete them — they're yours. One level of nesting lets you roll detail up: a Shopping parent can hold Clothes, Electronics, and Accessories, and reports total the children under the parent.

The microloan book, kept simple

Lending money out is a business with its own system. Tally doesn't track individual loans — it holds a single manually-valued Microloans account standing for your deployed principal. Interest you receive is booked as ordinary income on the day it lands, so it never gets double-counted against the principal. Once a month you record a fresh valuation and the account's balance snaps to it.

Day to day

The handful of things you'll actually do

Most days it's one quick entry. Once a month there's a little upkeep. Exporting is there when you want your data elsewhere.

Record a transaction

  1. Open quick-add from anywhere
  2. Pick expense, income, or transfer
  3. Enter the amount and account
  4. Choose a category (not for transfers)
  5. Save — the dashboard updates

Monthly valuation

  1. Read the balance from your loan or investment system
  2. Open the valued account
  3. Record a snapshot for the date
  4. Net worth reflects the new value

Export to CSV

  1. Filter the transactions you want
  2. Trigger export
  3. Download a clean RFC-4180 file
  4. Money stays exact, as strings

Reading it back

What the dashboard actually tells you

Every figure here is arithmetic on what you recorded — a computed fact, never a recommendation. Tally has no advisor and no smart insights; it just does the sums faithfully.

Net worth

Everything you hold, in one line

The sum of flow-derived balances and the latest snapshot of each valued account. Transfers between your own accounts never move it.

Savings rate

How much of your income you kept

Income minus expenses, as a share of income. "Kept 41% this month" is a derived stat — plain division, not a nudge.

Spending by category

Where it went, rolled up

Expenses grouped by category for the period, with child categories totalled under their parent so the picture stays legible.

Spotting losses

What's quietly shrinking

Valued accounts trending down across snapshots surface the losses you asked Tally to help you find in the first place.
